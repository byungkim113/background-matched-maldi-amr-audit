\begin{abstract}
Machine learning applied to matrix-assisted laser desorption/ionization time-of-flight (MALDI-TOF) mass spectra can predict antimicrobial resistance (AMR) with clinically relevant accuracy, yet the biological basis of successful predictions remains poorly understood. Resistant bacteria are not randomly distributed across clinical populations: resistance is frequently carried by disseminated clonal lineages, co-resistance blocks and hospital-specific ecological mixtures. When a MALDI-TOF model generalizes across sites, it may do so by detecting the spectral signature of a resistant subpopulation rather than the focal resistance biology. We introduce a model-agnostic background-matched audit that tests whether isolate-level predictions survive after controlling for co-resistance background. Applied to an expanded \textit{Escherichia coli} DRIAMS panel spanning four sites and six drugs, ciprofloxacin predictions retained within-background discrimination at temporal and external sites (background-centered AUC 0.703, 0.646 and 0.596 at A-2018, DRIAMS-C and DRIAMS-D), whereas amoxicillin-clavulanic acid weakened or collapsed toward chance (0.541, 0.497 and 0.486). A co-resistance-only baseline predictor matched or exceeded MALDI CNN performance for amoxicillin-clavulanic acid at all external sites, whereas \textit{S.~aureus}/oxacillin predictions in a second-organism generality check exceeded the co-resistance-only baseline and retained within-background signal at A-2018 (background-centred AUC 0.763, 9 strata) and DRIAMS-C (0.699, 2 strata). The same attenuation appeared in both convolutional neural network and multi-task LightGBM outputs, indicating that background sensitivity reflects the relationship between spectra and resistant-population structure, not a single architectural choice. Cross-resistance networks revealed strong co-resistance blocks, including ciprofloxacin/norfloxacin ($\phi = 0.976$) and extended-spectrum cephalosporins ($\phi = 0.804$--$0.884$). In an independent public WGS-linked Basel uropathogenic \textit{E.~coli} cohort, MALDI peak features predicted ST131 lineage more strongly (AUC 0.906) than ciprofloxacin (0.739) or ceftriaxone resistance (0.650). Exact sequence-type control attenuated ciprofloxacin AUC to 0.564 and ceftriaxone AUC to 0.503, while resistance-associated discriminative peaks remained enriched for published ST131 MALDI biomarker masses. Background-matched evaluation provides a practical and model-agnostic guardrail for interpreting MALDI-TOF AMR performance before clinical deployment.
\end{abstract}

\section*{Introduction}

Rapid determination of antimicrobial susceptibility is critical for guiding appropriate treatment and stewardship in bacterial infection. MALDI-TOF mass spectrometry is already deployed in clinical microbiology laboratories for organism identification at low cost and high throughput, and this infrastructure has motivated sustained interest in whether the same spectra can support AMR prediction. The DRIAMS resource demonstrated that MALDI-TOF AMR models can achieve clinically meaningful performance across multiple organism-drug pairs and hospital sites\cite{weis2022driams,driamsdryad}, and subsequent work has extended these findings to multi-drug panels and alternative model architectures. However, the biological basis of successful cross-site predictions has received far less scrutiny than performance optimization.

The central difficulty is that a resistant label is not an isolated molecular state. In clinical bacterial populations, resistance often co-segregates with clonal lineage, mobile genetic elements, co-resistance burdens and hospital-specific ecological pressures. A machine-learning model trained to separate resistant from susceptible isolates can, in principle, exploit any spectral correlate of resistance, including lineage-associated protein peaks that travel with resistant subpopulations and are entirely independent of the focal resistance mechanism. Routine MALDI-TOF acquisition captures abundant cytoplasmic, ribosomal and outer-membrane proteins in the 2--20\,kDa range; clonal lineages accumulate protein-coding polymorphisms over evolutionary time that generate lineage-specific mass patterns entirely unrelated to antibiotic resistance. These patterns can be highly consistent within a clone yet divergent between clones that share identical resistance phenotypes, providing a spectrally detectable lineage signal that a classifier trained to predict resistance may inadvertently learn to exploit. Recent analysis of whole-genome-sequence-based AMR prediction has shown that population structure systematically biases performance estimates when sample collection is phylogenetically non-random\cite{yu2025population}. For MALDI-TOF models, the analogous risk has been discussed qualitatively\cite{weis2022stratification}, but a practical, model-agnostic method for quantifying its contribution has been absent.

The common evaluation question --- can a model predict resistance? --- is insufficient for clinical deployment. A more demanding question is whether model predictions reflect the focal drug-resistance relationship specifically, or whether the reported performance partially reflects the spectral structure of the resistant bacterial population that happened to be present in the training sites. A model can have high external AUC while drawing signal from co-resistance background, and that signal may not transfer reliably when the resistant subpopulation composition changes across hospitals. Without a method to separate these contributions, an AUC figure alone cannot distinguish portable focal signal from background-driven transfer.

Here we develop and apply a Background-Matched MALDI-AMR Audit that directly tests the survivability of model predictions inside matched co-resistance backgrounds. The method requires only a standard prediction table --- isolate identifier, site, focal drug, binary AST label and model probability --- and is therefore applicable to any MALDI-TOF AMR model. We show that the degree of survival varies systematically and predictably: \textit{E.~coli} ciprofloxacin predictions, which travel with a globally disseminated fluoroquinolone-resistant clonal lineage, retain more within-background signal than \textit{E.~coli} amoxicillin-clavulanic acid predictions, where resistance is ecologically heterogeneous. Cross-resistance network analysis, model-family replication, and independent public WGS-linked MALDI data together support the interpretation that MALDI-TOF AMR models are sensitive to resistant-population background, and that this sensitivity is the primary driver of the observed cross-site performance variation.

\section*{Results}

\subsection*{Raw external performance varies markedly across organism-drug pairs and sites}

As a baseline, we evaluated CNN predictions for the expanded \textit{E.~coli} panel across four evaluation contexts: the temporal within-site DRIAMS-A 2018 hold-out and three external hospital sites, DRIAMS-B, DRIAMS-C and DRIAMS-D. Across all six drugs in the panel, raw AUC at the temporal A-2018 split ranged from 0.650 (amoxicillin-clavulanic acid) to 0.867 (cefepime), with ciprofloxacin at 0.823 and extended-spectrum cephalosporins at 0.863 (ceftriaxone), 0.838 (ceftazidime) and 0.867 (cefepime). At DRIAMS-C and DRIAMS-D, performance declined across the panel but remained above chance for most drug-site pairs. Ciprofloxacin raw AUC was 0.750 and 0.671 at DRIAMS-C and DRIAMS-D, respectively; amoxicillin-clavulanic acid raw AUC was 0.535 and 0.557.

Taken at face value, these figures appear to support moderate cross-site portability for the full drug panel. However, raw AUC treats all isolates as exchangeable regardless of their co-resistance context. A model that scores resistant isolates highly because they carry a co-resistance background associated with higher mean model output --- rather than because the model detects focal drug-resistance biology --- will produce elevated raw AUC whenever the training and test populations share a similar co-resistance ecology. Conversely, when the resistant-population background at the test site differs from the training site, raw AUC will decline in a way that is indistinguishable, from the AUC figure alone, from a genuine loss of focal drug signal. The audit we develop next is designed to separate these contributions.

\subsection*{A model-agnostic audit for resistant-population background}

The background-matched audit operates on a flat prediction table containing isolate identifier, site, year, organism, focal drug, binary AST label and model probability. For each focal drug, a co-resistance background signature is constructed from the binary AST calls of the remaining drugs in the panel. Isolates sharing the same background signature form a stratum. We retain strata containing at least three resistant and three susceptible isolates for the focal drug, and compute four quantities (Fig.~\ref{fig:framework}). Raw AUC measures ordinary external performance without any background control. Matched AUC restricts computation to valid co-resistance strata, testing whether performance holds on the identifiable subset of the data. Background-centered AUC replaces each score with its residual after subtracting the mean score of its background stratum, then computes one pooled AUC over retained isolates. This is a stratum-adjusted AUC: within-stratum resistant-susceptible comparisons are directly background-controlled, whereas cross-stratum comparisons are adjusted for mean score shifts but can still contribute residual distributional differences. We therefore also report pairwise within-background accuracy, which counts only resistant-susceptible pairs drawn from the same valid stratum. Pairwise accuracy is the most direct test of whether focal-drug signal survives inside matched backgrounds, while background-centered AUC provides a higher-powered pooled summary.

\subsection*{Co-resistance-only baselines quantify AST-label shortcut structure}

A predictor that ignores MALDI spectra and predicts the focal drug from co-resistance AST labels alone sets a minimum performance floor that any MALDI model must exceed to claim spectral value. We computed an exact-background co-resistance-only baseline by scoring each isolate's leave-one-out smoothed resistance prevalence within its exact background signature (Fig.~\ref{fig:three-way}; see Methods).

For extended-spectrum cephalosporins at DRIAMS-B and DRIAMS-C, the co-resistance-only baseline achieved AUC of 0.997--1.000 for ceftriaxone, ceftazidime and cefepime. For norfloxacin at A-2018, co-resistance-only AUC was 0.9999. These near-perfect values follow directly from the near-perfect phi correlations among these drugs in the panel (Fig.~\ref{fig:cross-resistance}): because norfloxacin resistance and cephalosporin resistance co-segregate at near-unity rates with ciprofloxacin and the ESBL block respectively, knowing the co-resistance profile almost completely determines the focal drug label. In this setting, MALDI models that achieve high external AUC for these drugs are largely recovering AST label correlation, not spectral biology.

For \textit{E.~coli} amoxicillin-clavulanic acid at DRIAMS-C, the co-resistance-only baseline (AUC 0.687) exceeded the CNN (AUC 0.535), demonstrating that the model's prediction at this site was quantitatively worse than a no-spectrum predictor. The pattern held at DRIAMS-B as well (co-resistance-only 0.796 vs CNN 0.694). At DRIAMS-D, the co-resistance-only baseline (0.522) was close to chance, and CNN (0.557) marginally exceeded it but without substantial gain.

In contrast, for \textit{E.~coli} ciprofloxacin at DRIAMS-D, the CNN (AUC 0.671) exceeded the co-resistance-only baseline (AUC 0.522) by 0.149 AUC units --- the largest MALDI-over-background margin in the panel. This is also the site where the stratum-centred audit confirmed the most residual within-background signal (centred AUC 0.596). The convergence of these two measures at the same site, which serves a predominantly outpatient population where ST131 prevalence may differ from the university-hospital training sites, is consistent with the model detecting a partial fluoroquinolone-resistance signal that persists even when background ecology shifts.

The three-way decomposition --- raw MALDI AUC, co-resistance-only AUC, background-centred MALDI AUC --- is the most complete audit view (Fig.~\ref{fig:three-way}). The gap between raw AUC and co-resistance-only AUC measures how much of apparent performance reflects AST label correlation rather than spectral content. The background-centred AUC then tests whether whatever spectral content survives is focal rather than distributed across the co-resistance background.

\subsection*{Ciprofloxacin retains within-background signal; amoxicillin-clavulanic acid collapses}

The primary contrast compares \textit{E.~coli}/ciprofloxacin with \textit{E.~coli}/amoxicillin-clavulanic acid across the same training pipeline, organism, and model architecture. This contrast is informative because the two drugs differ substantially in the ecology of their resistant populations. Ciprofloxacin resistance in \textit{E.~coli} is tightly associated with globally disseminated fluoroquinolone-resistant lineages, most prominently sequence type 131 (ST131), which carries chromosomal \textit{gyrA}/\textit{parC} mutations across geographic settings\cite{nicolas2014st131}. Amoxicillin-clavulanic acid resistance is more ecologically heterogeneous, driven by diverse plasmid-borne ESBL and inhibitor-resistance mechanisms distributed across multiple \textit{E.~coli} lineages.

After background centering, ciprofloxacin retained residual discrimination at all primary evaluation contexts: centered AUC was 0.703 at A-2018, 0.646 at DRIAMS-C and 0.596 at DRIAMS-D (Fig.~\ref{fig:primary-audit}; Table~\ref{tab:primary-audit}). The direct pairwise within-background statistic showed the same direction: within matched co-resistance strata, ciprofloxacin-resistant isolates were ranked above susceptible isolates with accuracy 0.754 at A-2018, 0.675 at DRIAMS-C and 0.616 at DRIAMS-D. DRIAMS-B produced a high centered estimate and high pairwise accuracy but with low matched support and is treated as cautionary. In contrast, amoxicillin-clavulanic acid showed a markedly different pattern. At DRIAMS-C and DRIAMS-D, centered AUC fell to 0.497 and 0.486, and pairwise within-background accuracy fell below chance (0.484 and 0.471), despite high matched retention. At A-2018 and DRIAMS-B, centered AUC was 0.541 and 0.576, with pairwise accuracy 0.546 and 0.576, indicating weak residual signal rather than robust focal discrimination. The raw-to-centered drop, together with the direct pairwise statistic, was substantially less favorable for amoxicillin-clavulanic acid than for ciprofloxacin at every site with adequate matched support (Table~\ref{tab:primary-audit}).

This pattern is the core evidence of the framework. The audit does not require knowledge of the true clone or resistance gene. It asks a narrower and more tractable question: if two isolates carry the same co-resistance profile, can the model still rank the resistant isolate above the susceptible isolate for the focal drug? Pairwise within-background accuracy answers that question directly; background-centered AUC provides a pooled stratum-adjusted companion statistic. For ciprofloxacin, both statistics remain above chance at the main interpretable sites, though attenuated from raw performance. For amoxicillin-clavulanic acid at external sites, both statistics are weak, chance-level, or below chance.

\subsection*{\textit{S.~aureus}/oxacillin retained signal in a second-organism generality check}

The \textit{E.~coli} contrast establishes the framework on a single organism. To test whether background-sensitive attenuation and focal-signal retention are organism-specific or reflect a more general property, we applied the identical audit to CNN/multi-task predictions for \textit{S.~aureus}/oxacillin across three DRIAMS sites (A-2018, DRIAMS-B, DRIAMS-C). This analysis is a second-organism generality check, not a full multi-drug replication; the \textit{S.~aureus} panel is evaluated independently and should not be aggregated with the \textit{E.~coli} results.

Background-centred AUC for \textit{S.~aureus}/oxacillin was 0.763 (95\% CI 0.714--0.808; 9 valid strata) at A-2018 and 0.699 (95\% CI 0.615--0.782; 2 valid strata) at DRIAMS-C, both above chance with permutation $p < 0.01$ (Supplementary Table~3). DRIAMS-B produced a higher point estimate (0.864, 1 valid stratum) but with sparse matched support and is treated as cautionary. Applying the $n \geq 10$ sensitivity threshold, A-2018 remained interpretable (centred AUC 0.766, 3 strata) and DRIAMS-C was retained (0.723, 1 stratum); DRIAMS-B dropped out entirely at this threshold, confirming that the DRIAMS-B estimate is not a load-bearing result. CNN raw AUC exceeded the co-resistance-only baseline by 0.076 at A-2018 and 0.098 at DRIAMS-C (Fig.~\ref{fig:three-way}), consistent with the model capturing spectral information beyond AST label correlation at the interpretable sites.

The mechanistic context for \textit{S.~aureus}/oxacillin retention differs from \textit{E.~coli}/ciprofloxacin. Oxacillin resistance in \textit{S.~aureus} is mediated by PBP2a, encoded by mecA, a high-molecular-weight transpeptidase ($\sim$76\,kDa) outside the routine 2--20\,kDa acquisition window and not directly detected by whole-cell MALDI-TOF. However, MRSA strains accumulate broad proteomic differences relative to methicillin-susceptible \textit{S.~aureus} across the detectable mass range, consistent with the extensive transcriptional and metabolic remodelling associated with mecA acquisition and the clonal population structure of dominant healthcare MRSA lineages. The background-matched audit confirms that these population-level spectral differences are partially captured within matched co-resistance strata at A-2018 and DRIAMS-C. The result does not implicate any specific protein but establishes that focal-signal retention is not unique to the fluoroquinolone-resistance ecology.

The Weis-style logistic regression applied to the official published \textit{S.~aureus} panel yielded weaker matched support (1 valid stratum at DRIAMS-C; centred AUC 0.543), reflecting the reduced discriminative power of a restricted co-resistance panel with fewer background dimensions. This is reported as a published-workflow compatibility check and is not the primary Sa/Oxa result; the CNN/Mega audit above is the main evidence.

\subsection*{Background sensitivity persists across model families}

A natural concern is that background sensitivity might be an artifact specific to the convolutional architecture. To address this, we applied the identical audit to CNN predictions and to multi-task LightGBM predictions trained on the same data splits. The goal was not to identify a superior model but to test whether raw-to-centered attenuation is architecture-specific or characteristic of the data relationship itself.

Across the primary \textit{E.~coli}/ciprofloxacin and amoxicillin-clavulanic acid comparisons, CNN and multi-task LightGBM showed the same qualitative behavior (Supplementary~Fig.~1; Table~\ref{tab:model-replication}). Ciprofloxacin retained centered AUC more consistently than amoxicillin-clavulanic acid in both model families. Amoxicillin-clavulanic acid collapsed at DRIAMS-C and DRIAMS-D in both model families despite high matched retention. The magnitude of the raw-to-centered drop was quantitatively similar for CNN and LightGBM, which encode the MALDI spectrum through entirely different inductive biases. This consistency supports the interpretation that background sensitivity originates in the co-structure of spectra, AST labels and resistant-population ecology, not in a particular representational choice. An expanded model-class comparison including single-task LightGBM and the \textit{S.~aureus}/oxacillin model family is reported in Supplementary Table~4.

\subsection*{Resistance labels form exploitable co-resistance blocks}

The audit rests on an empirical premise: resistance labels are not independent, and a model that predicts one drug may exploit the spectral correlates of a co-resistance block shared by several drugs. We tested this directly by computing pairwise co-resistance statistics from the isolate-level AST labels in the expanded \textit{E.~coli} panel.

The strongest co-resistance edge was ciprofloxacin/norfloxacin ($\phi = 0.976$; resistant Jaccard = 0.963), showing that these two fluoroquinolones overwhelmingly identify the same resistant subpopulation in clinical DRIAMS data. The near-perfect concordance reflects the shared mechanism underlying both resistance phenotypes: fluoroquinolone resistance in \textit{E.~coli} is predominantly mediated by chromosomal \textit{gyrA}/\textit{parC} mutations that confer simultaneous ciprofloxacin and norfloxacin resistance, and is concentrated in the ST131 lineage and its fluoroquinolone-resistant relatives. The next strongest edges spanned extended-spectrum cephalosporins: ceftriaxone/ceftazidime ($\phi = 0.884$), ceftazidime/cefepime ($\phi = 0.828$) and ceftriaxone/cefepime ($\phi = 0.804$) (Supplementary~Fig.~2; Table~\ref{tab:cross-resistance}). This cephalosporin block reflects the co-dissemination of ESBLs, principally CTX-M-type enzymes, on conjugative plasmids distributed across multiple \textit{E.~coli} lineages; isolates carrying these plasmids tend to be resistant to the full range of extended-spectrum cephalosporins and are further over-represented in clinical collections because they are enriched during selective antibiotic pressure. The co-resistance between the fluoroquinolone and cephalosporin blocks is itself substantial, because ESBL-carrying ST131 clade C isolates frequently co-harbour fluoroquinolone resistance, creating a combined ecological structure in which fluoroquinolone-resistant and ESBL-producing isolates substantially overlap.

These blocks are the ecological structures that a MALDI-TOF model can inadvertently exploit. A model trained to predict cefepime resistance, for example, faces a training set in which nearly all cefepime-resistant isolates are also ceftriaxone- and ceftazidime-resistant, sharing a common lineage ecology. Discriminative spectral features learned in this setting may reflect ESBL-carrying lineage identity rather than any cefepime-specific susceptibility biology, and will transfer to external sites only to the degree that the ESBL-carrying subpopulation composition is preserved. The cross-resistance network makes this hazard explicit and quantifiable before model evaluation.

\subsection*{Public WGS-linked MALDI data demonstrate stronger lineage than resistance encoding}

The background-matched audit is designed for clinical datasets that lack WGS labels. To evaluate whether the underlying biological concern is plausible --- that is, whether MALDI spectra encode lineage identity more strongly than focal resistance --- we analysed an independent public Basel/Cu\'{e}nod uropathogenic \textit{E.~coli} resource that links WGS-derived metadata to Bruker MALDI peak features\cite{cuenod2023papgii}. This cohort is epidemiologically relevant: it comprises prospectively collected uropathogenic \textit{E.~coli} from a tertiary-care setting in which ST131 is the dominant fluoroquinolone-resistant lineage, closely mirroring the clinical context of the DRIAMS panel. After joining public metadata and Bruker median-peak tables, 407 isolates had usable peak features; 55 carried the ST131 sequence type by WGS.

A centroid-direction classifier applied to binned MALDI peak features predicted ST131 lineage with AUC 0.906 (Fig.~\ref{fig:public-support}a; Table~\ref{tab:public-wgs}). In the same dataset and with the same classification approach, ciprofloxacin resistance was predicted with AUC 0.739 and ceftriaxone resistance with AUC 0.650. The gap between ST131 lineage prediction (0.906) and ciprofloxacin resistance prediction (0.739) indicates that MALDI spectra encode enough lineage-associated protein variation to separate ST131 from non-ST131 isolates with high accuracy. Because ST131 is the dominant ciprofloxacin-resistant \textit{E.~coli} lineage globally, a MALDI-TOF model that exploits lineage identity would naturally produce elevated AUC for ciprofloxacin resistance as a secondary consequence, without directly detecting any fluoroquinolone resistance mechanism.

We next used the WGS metadata to ask whether resistance prediction survives explicit lineage control. Ciprofloxacin retained residual signal inside ST131 isolates (AUC 0.725) and outside ST131 isolates (AUC 0.644), but exact sequence-type centering reduced the overall ciprofloxacin AUC from 0.739 to 0.564, with only 36\% of isolates retained in sequence types containing both resistant and susceptible isolates (Table~\ref{tab:upec-clone-control}). Ceftriaxone resistance was more strongly lineage-associated: within ST131 isolates AUC was 0.515, and exact sequence-type centering reduced AUC from 0.650 to 0.503. Broad phylogroup control did not explain the ciprofloxacin signal (phylogroup-centered AUC 0.762), indicating that the relevant background axis is fine-grained sequence type rather than broad phylogroup. These WGS-linked controls provide independent support for the audit's premise: MALDI spectra can encode clonal background strongly enough that resistance performance must be interpreted against lineage and co-resistance structure.

\subsection*{Resistance-associated discriminative peaks overlap published ST131 biomarkers}

We next asked whether discriminative MALDI peak bins for resistance phenotypes are enriched for the same published ST131 MALDI biomarker masses identified in independent proteomics work, or whether they occupy distinct spectral regions\cite{nakamura2019st131}. Among the top 75 discriminative bins per target, overlaps with published ST131 biomarker masses exceeded a mass-stratified permutation null at all three targets: 14 of 75 ST131 bins overlapped (3.11-fold enrichment, empirical $p = 0.0001$), 10 of 75 ciprofloxacin resistance bins overlapped (2.24-fold, $p = 0.0068$), and 12 of 75 ceftriaxone resistance bins overlapped (2.64-fold, $p = 0.0006$) (Fig.~\ref{fig:public-support}b; Table~\ref{tab:biomarker-enrichment}).

\input{tables/table_5_biomarker_enrichment.tex}

This overlap does not identify specific proteins, which would require MS/MS or LC-MS/MS validation. This distinction is important because the enrichment test uses binned peak neighborhoods, and some individual mass-neighborhood matches fall more than 25\,Da from the published biomarker mass. For routine whole-cell clinical MALDI-TOF acquired in linear mode, this is too loose for protein identity claims, particularly for intact proteins in the 4--12\,kDa range. We therefore interpret the analysis only as enrichment of discriminative spectral neighborhoods, and tier individual candidate correspondences as high-confidence ($\leq 10$\,Da), putative (10--25\,Da) or loose ($>25$\,Da; listed for completeness only). Under this conservative interpretation, the mass spectral regions most discriminative for resistance phenotypes remain non-randomly enriched for the same regions that independently separate ST131 from non-ST131 isolates. The enrichment is coherent with the hypothesis that resistance-discriminative peaks in MALDI-TOF spectra partially reflect lineage-associated protein variation rather than direct detection of resistance-specific proteins, many of which fall at or above the practical routine MALDI-TOF acquisition range.

\section*{Discussion}

This study reframes MALDI-TOF AMR evaluation from a performance-recovery question to a background-decomposition question. A model may achieve useful raw external AUC while drawing a substantial fraction of its signal from the spectral signature of the resistant bacterial population rather than from the focal drug-resistance relationship. The Background-Matched MALDI-AMR Audit makes this decomposition quantitative, model-agnostic, and applicable to any dataset with isolate-level predictions and co-drug AST labels. Its two central outputs are complementary. Pairwise within-background accuracy is the cleanest test of focal signal because it counts only resistant-susceptible pairs from the same co-resistance stratum. Background-centered AUC is a higher-powered pooled statistic that removes stratum-level mean score shifts before recomputing AUC; it is stricter than raw cross-site AUC, but it is not a pure within-stratum statistic because cross-stratum residual comparisons still contribute to the pooled AUC. We therefore interpret strong evidence for focal signal only when centered AUC, pairwise accuracy and matched retention point in the same direction.

The principal finding is the \textit{E.~coli} ciprofloxacin versus amoxicillin-clavulanic acid contrast. Ciprofloxacin predictions retain within-background ranking after co-resistance control: centered AUC remains above chance at all three primary evaluation contexts, declining from 0.703 at the temporal A-2018 hold-out to 0.596 at DRIAMS-D. Amoxicillin-clavulanic acid predictions weaken or collapse to chance at external sites with high matched retention, despite initially plausible raw AUC figures. This contrast aligns with the known ecology of resistance in \textit{E.~coli}. Ciprofloxacin resistance is heavily concentrated in ST131 and closely related fluoroquinolone-resistant lineages that carry a consistent chromosomal mutation profile and an associated proteomic signature that is conserved across geographies\cite{nicolas2014st131}. Amoxicillin-clavulanic acid resistance reflects heterogeneous beta-lactamase and beta-lactamase-inhibitor combinations --- most prominently CTX-M-type ESBLs on conjugative plasmids --- distributed across a diverse collection of \textit{E.~coli} lineages with no single dominant background. A MALDI-TOF model predicting amoxicillin-clavulanic acid resistance therefore encounters resistant isolates with heterogeneous protein profiles that do not form a coherent spectral subpopulation, and the signal it captures at the source site may not reproduce when the local ESBL-carrying lineage composition changes. The audit does not prove that the DRIAMS CNN detects ST131 directly; it shows that ciprofloxacin predictions contain residual information about the resistant isolate even within a co-resistance-matched group, while amoxicillin-clavulanic acid predictions largely do not. These two outcomes represent biologically interpretable endpoints of a spectrum: at one end, resistance is dominated by a single globally disseminated clone carrying a spectrally coherent signature; at the other, resistance is distributed across ecologically diverse backgrounds with no stable spectral anchor.

The divergence between raw and background-centered AUC reveals a more fundamental interpretive problem: source-site performance does not reliably predict which drug predictions will survive background matching at external sites. Amoxicillin-clavulanic acid achieved a source-site raw AUC of 0.687 at A-2018, approaching the 0.823 achieved by ciprofloxacin, yet it was the drug whose predictions most consistently collapsed to chance after background centering at external sites with adequate matched support. This decorrelation is not incidental to sample size or model variance; it reflects the fact that source-site AUC is determined jointly by the focal drug signal and the background ecology of the training population. A model that exploits co-resistance structure at the training site will appear to generalize externally whenever the receiving site happens to carry a similar background structure, and will fail without warning from the source-site AUC when that structure changes. The expected direction of this failure is not random: drugs whose resistance is concentrated in a single dominant lineage at the training site will tend to show the largest source-to-external AUC decline when deployed to sites where that lineage is proportionally less common, not because the model has degraded but because the spectrally coherent resistant subpopulation it tracks has changed its relative frequency. The present results, particularly the DRIAMS-D decline for ciprofloxacin, are consistent with this interpretation: DRIAMS-D is operated by a community laboratory serving a predominantly outpatient population in which the relative frequency of ST131 among fluoroquinolone-resistant isolates may differ from the university hospital settings of the training sites. Standard deployment triage, which filters models on held-out source performance, cannot detect this vulnerability, whereas the combined audit outputs quantify the risk before commitment.

The model-family replication strengthens these interpretations. The qualitative pattern --- ciprofloxacin retaining, amoxicillin-clavulanic acid collapsing --- appears in both the CNN and the multi-task LightGBM, which differ entirely in architecture, regularisation and inductive bias. The CNN learns distributed spectral representations through convolutional filters; the LightGBM selects discrete peak-bin features through gradient-boosted decision trees. That two such architecturally distant models produce the same background-sensitivity pattern, and produce it with quantitatively similar raw-to-centered drops, indicates that the phenomenon originates in the alignment between spectral features and co-resistance ecology in the DRIAMS data, not in any representational choice made by the model designer. This parallels findings in WGS-based AMR prediction, where population-structure confounding produces similar performance inflations across logistic regression, random forests and neural networks trained on the same data\cite{yu2025population}. The present work extends this observation to MALDI-TOF spectra and demonstrates that the confound is detectable without WGS labels, using only the co-drug AST calls already present in routine susceptibility panels.

The public WGS-linked analyses provide independent biological grounding that does not rely on any property of the DRIAMS-trained model. In 407 uropathogenic \textit{E.~coli} isolates with matched Bruker MALDI peak features and WGS-derived sequence type assignments, ST131 lineage identity was predictable from MALDI peaks at AUC 0.906 --- substantially more accurately than ciprofloxacin (0.739) or ceftriaxone resistance (0.650) in the same cohort. This gap is the central biological support for the audit framework. If routine MALDI-TOF spectra encode enough lineage-associated protein variation to separate ST131 from non-ST131 isolates with near-excellent accuracy, then a model trained to classify ciprofloxacin resistance in a population where ST131 is the dominant resistant lineage is presented with a high-quality lineage-detection shortcut.

The clone-controlled UPEC analysis sharpens this point. Ciprofloxacin resistance remained partially predictable within ST131 and non-ST131 subsets, but exact sequence-type centering attenuated AUC from 0.739 to 0.564. Ceftriaxone resistance, by contrast, collapsed to chance inside ST131 isolates and after exact sequence-type centering. Thus, the public WGS-linked data do not merely show that lineage is detectable; they show that resistance prediction itself is reduced when fine-grained lineage background is controlled. Whether and to what degree the DRIAMS-trained model exploits the same shortcut cannot be determined from these data alone, as the public UPEC cohort and DRIAMS are independent datasets with different collection contexts. The biomarker enrichment analysis reinforces the plausibility argument by showing that resistance-discriminative peak bins are non-randomly enriched for the same spectral regions that independently identify ST131-associated proteins. Together, these results support the inference that at least some MALDI-AMR signal reflects lineage-associated spectral variation, although rigorous quantification in the primary clinical benchmark requires WGS labels linked to DRIAMS isolates.

The broader MALDI-TOF AMR evaluation literature has not yet incorporated background-aware decomposition as a standard step. The original DRIAMS benchmark used random cross-validation as the primary within-source evaluation, which distributes isolates from the same clonal lineages across training and test folds and systematically overestimates within-source AUC relative to temporally stratified evaluation. Hierarchical stratification approaches have begun to address this by partitioning test sets along spectral-similarity clusters\cite{weis2022stratification}, but rely on pre-computed spectral distance matrices that are generally unavailable for prospective evaluation and do not directly report how much performance survives within each background stratum. Deep learning transfer approaches, including domain-adversarial training and fine-tuning on target-site isolates, have substantially improved reported cross-site AUC, but the latter strategy re-introduces the local resistant-population distribution as a training signal and conflates adaptation to site ecology with generalisation of focal resistance biology. The background-matched audit occupies a distinct niche in this landscape. It requires no spectral preprocessing, no phylogenetic data, no retraining and no labeled target-site isolates. It operates on any fixed model output and produces per-drug, per-site summaries that separate ordinary raw AUC from exact within-background pairwise ranking and pooled stratum-adjusted AUC. This framing makes the audit applicable not only to prospective model evaluation but to retrospective reanalysis of any prediction table from any previously published MALDI-TOF AMR study, and enables direct comparisons across model families and datasets in a common currency.

Several limitations circumscribe the scope of the present conclusions. First, co-resistance background is an imperfect proxy for phylogenetic background. A matched co-resistance profile constrains but does not uniquely determine the underlying lineage, and isolates with identical AST profiles can differ in clonal background or plasmid content. WGS or MLST data linked directly to DRIAMS isolates would enable a more rigorous partitioning of focal and lineage-associated signal, and this remains a priority for future work. Second, background-centered AUC is a pooled stratum-adjusted statistic rather than a purely within-stratum statistic; cross-stratum comparisons still enter the AUC after mean centering. We therefore report pairwise within-background accuracy alongside centered AUC and matched retention, and recommend the same practice in all audit applications. Third, some organism-drug-site combinations have low matched retention after stratum filtering, and results from these contexts are flagged as cautionary throughout. Fourth, the enrichment of discriminative peak bins for published biomarker masses is a mass-neighborhood analysis, not protein identification. Matches more than 25\,Da from a published biomarker mass are too loose for identity claims and are treated only as broad spectral-neighborhood overlaps; assigning candidate identities to enriched bins would require MS/MS fragmentation, MALDI-TOF/TOF or LC-MS/MS. Fifth, the Weis-style logistic regression audit, which conditions on specific co-resistance predictors as fixed effects rather than performing exact stratum matching, currently rests on a prediction export covering a subset of external isolates and should be treated as supplementary evidence pending a full-row rerun with all available prediction rows. Sixth, the second-organism generality check for \textit{S.~aureus}/oxacillin covers three sites and a single drug and does not constitute systematic multi-organism coverage; whether equivalent patterns appear for \textit{Klebsiella pneumoniae}, \textit{Enterococcus faecium} and additional \textit{S.~aureus} drug--site combinations remains an open priority for future work.

Two extensions are most consequential for resolving the outstanding uncertainties. First, direct linkage of WGS or MLST data to DRIAMS isolates would allow the same sequence-type-centered decomposition performed in the public UPEC cohort to be applied directly to the primary clinical benchmark, determining what fraction, if any, of ciprofloxacin signal is genuinely independent of lineage identity in DRIAMS. Second, organism-drug pairs in which resistance is mediated by mechanisms not concentrated in a dominant lineage --- constitutive chromosomal AmpC expression in non-clonal \textit{E.~coli}, for example, or intrinsic resistance phenotypes broadly distributed across the species population --- represent critical positive controls for the audit framework. If background-centered AUC is retained for these pairs, the result would establish that background sensitivity is not a universal property of MALDI-TOF AMR models but a function of the ecological concentration of resistance in the training population. Such a positive-control result would substantially strengthen the case for using background-centered AUC as a deployment criterion and would clarify which organism-drug combinations are most and least susceptible to the confound.

The practical recommendation follows directly from these findings: MALDI-TOF AMR studies and regulatory evaluations should report background-centered AUC, pairwise within-background accuracy, matched retention and a cross-resistance network alongside raw AUC as a minimum standard for external validation. A model intended to support treatment decisions for a given drug should demonstrate that its predictions survive co-resistance control at the intended deployment site, not merely that they transfer in aggregate. The Background-Matched MALDI-AMR Audit is portable, requires no additional data beyond routine AST panels and fixed model predictions, and produces outputs that are interpretable at the level of individual drug-site pairs by clinical microbiologists and infectious disease specialists alike. We release the complete audit engine and all analysis code to facilitate adoption as a standard evaluation complement to raw cross-site AUC in future MALDI-TOF AMR research and clinical translation.

\section*{Methods}

\subsection*{DRIAMS datasets and organism-drug panels}

We used DRIAMS MALDI-TOF spectra and AST labels as the primary clinical benchmark\cite{weis2022driams,driamsdryad}. Models were trained on DRIAMS-A isolates collected before 2018 (approximately 90\% train, 10\% within-source validation) and evaluated on a temporal hold-out of DRIAMS-A 2018 isolates and on the three geographically external sites DRIAMS-B, DRIAMS-C and DRIAMS-D. No labels or calibration thresholds from external sites were used during training or model selection. The expanded \textit{E.~coli} panel comprised ciprofloxacin, norfloxacin, amoxicillin-clavulanic acid, ceftriaxone, ceftazidime and cefepime. Intermediate AST calls and isolates with missing labels for the focal drug were excluded from the corresponding drug-level evaluation.

\subsection*{Prediction models}

The primary neural model was a multi-task CNN trained from DRIAMS MALDI-TOF spectra represented in the 2,000--20,000\,m/z range, as described in the companion code repository. The LightGBM baseline used peak-bin features from the same spectral range and was trained in a multi-task configuration on the same splits. The purpose of the LightGBM comparison was replication: to test whether background sensitivity appears outside the CNN architecture, not to tune for peak performance.

\subsection*{Background signatures and stratum definition}

For each isolate and focal drug, a co-resistance background signature was the concatenation of binary AST calls (R, S or missing) for all non-focal drugs in the same organism panel. For example, when auditing ciprofloxacin, the background signature for each isolate encodes its norfloxacin, amoxicillin-clavulanic acid, ceftriaxone, ceftazidime and cefepime labels. A valid stratum must contain at least three resistant and three susceptible isolates for the focal drug. Strata not meeting this threshold are excluded from matched and centered AUC computation and counted toward the matched-retention statistic.

\subsection*{Background-centered AUC and pairwise within-background accuracy}

For each site, organism and focal drug, raw AUC was computed from all available prediction rows. Matched AUC was computed after restriction to valid strata. For background-centered AUC, each prediction score $s_{ig}$ for isolate $i$ in background stratum $g$ was replaced with the residual $s'_{ig}=s_{ig}-\bar{s}_g$, where $\bar{s}_g$ is the mean model score among retained isolates in stratum $g$. AUC was then recomputed from these residual scores across all valid strata. This statistic removes stratum-level mean score shifts but remains a pooled AUC: within-stratum resistant-susceptible comparisons are directly controlled, whereas cross-stratum comparisons can still contribute after centering. We therefore also computed pairwise within-background accuracy, defined as the fraction of resistant-susceptible isolate pairs within the same valid stratum for which the resistant isolate received a higher model score than the susceptible isolate. Ties were counted as 0.5. Pairwise within-background accuracy is the direct within-stratum statistic; background-centered AUC is reported as a higher-powered stratum-adjusted companion.

\subsection*{Uncertainty estimation}

Bootstrap confidence intervals were computed by resampling prediction rows with replacement (500 replicates). Permutation p-values for the raw-to-centered drop were computed by shuffling focal-drug resistance labels within valid background strata and recomputing centered AUC (500 permutations). Pairwise within-background accuracy was not used as the primary permutation statistic because it has lower power in sparse strata; it is reported as the direct interpretability check for the same biological claim. Low-support rows are flagged in all tables using the adequacy thresholds described under stratum definition.

\subsection*{Co-resistance-only baseline}

For each focal drug, site and organism, we constructed a no-spectrum baseline predictor that scores each isolate by the leave-one-out smoothed resistance prevalence within its exact co-resistance background signature. Let $n_g$ be the number of isolates in background group $g$ (including the target isolate), $r_g$ the number resistant, and $\alpha = 2$ a Laplace smoothing parameter. The score for an isolate with focal label $y_i$ in group $g$ is $(r_g - y_i + \alpha/2) / (n_g - 1 + \alpha)$. This leave-one-out formulation prevents the isolate's own label from contributing to its score. AUC is computed from these scores using the same methodology as the main audit. A minimum of 30 isolates and at least 3 resistant isolates per background group are required for a site to be included; background groups with fewer than 30 observations are excluded from AUC computation. The resulting co-resistance-only AUC is the performance achievable from the AST co-resistance structure alone, without any access to MALDI spectra. The \textit{E.~coli} panel results are in \texttt{outputs/analysis\_outputs/co\_resistance\_only\_baseline\_ecoli/} and the \textit{S.~aureus} panel results are in \texttt{outputs/analysis\_outputs/co\_resistance\_only\_baseline\_saureus/}.

\subsection*{Cross-resistance network}

For each drug pair in the \textit{E.~coli} panel, we computed pairwise phi correlation, resistant-resistant lift and resistant Jaccard similarity from isolate-level binary AST calls, restricting to isolates with non-missing labels for both drugs. These statistics quantify the co-resistance blocks available for a MALDI-TOF model to exploit when trained on multi-drug panels.

\subsection*{Public WGS-linked UPEC analysis}

We used public Basel/Cu\'{e}nod uropathogenic \textit{E.~coli} resources linking WGS metadata and Bruker MALDI-derived median peak features\cite{cuenod2023papgii}. A total of 407 isolates had complete Bruker peak features and usable metadata after manifest joining; 55 carried the ST131 sequence type by WGS. We built 50\,Da-binned MALDI peak features from Bruker median peaks and evaluated a centroid-direction classifier for ST131 identity, ciprofloxacin resistance and ceftriaxone resistance using five cross-validation folds. To estimate lineage-controlled resistance signal, we repeated ciprofloxacin and ceftriaxone analyses inside ST131 and non-ST131 subsets and computed centered AUC after subtracting each WGS-derived background group's mean out-of-fold score. Background groups were ST131 status, exact sequence type and broad phylogroup. Exact sequence-type controls retain only sequence types containing both resistant and susceptible isolates for the focal drug; therefore, retained sample fractions are reported alongside centered AUC. This analysis is biologically motivating, not a deployment test of the DRIAMS-trained CNN.

\subsection*{Published ST131 biomarker enrichment}

Published ST131 MALDI biomarker masses were taken from the proteomics analysis of ESBL-producing \textit{E.~coli} ST131 by Nakamura \textit{et al.}\cite{nakamura2019st131}. For each classification target, we ranked MALDI bins by the absolute difference in mean log-intensity between positive and negative isolates and selected the top 75 bins. Overlap with the published biomarker list was assessed as a broad mass-neighborhood enrichment test using a 40\,Da window around each published biomarker mass. Statistical significance was evaluated against a mass-stratified permutation null: 1,000 random samples of 75 bins drawn with the same coarse m/z-stratum distribution as the target's discriminative bins, with overlap recomputed for each sample. Because clinical whole-cell MALDI-TOF spectra are commonly acquired in linear mode, individual matches larger than approximately 20--25\,Da are too loose to support protein identity. Candidate correspondences were therefore tiered as high-confidence ($\leq 10$\,Da), putative (10--25\,Da) and loose ($>25$\,Da; reported for completeness only). The enrichment analysis tests non-random overlap with published ST131 biomarker mass neighborhoods and does not identify specific proteins.

\subsection*{Software and reproducibility}

All analyses operate on a flat, model-agnostic prediction table with columns for isolate identifier, site, year, organism, drug, binary AST label and model probability. The main audit engine is \texttt{run\_background\_audit\_framework.py}. Publication figures and \LaTeX\ tables are generated by \texttt{scripts/make\_ncomms\_figures.py}. Any MALDI-TOF AMR model can be audited by exporting predictions in this format.
